The California Lemon Law is a state law that aims to protect consumers from defective cars. Passed in 1984, the law allows car buyers to return a defective vehicle within the first 18 months of ownership. The law also requires manufacturers to repair or replace defective vehicles, and to provide reimbursement for related expenses. The California Lemon Law is essential for consumer protection, and helps ensure that drivers can get their money's worth when they purchase a car.

How Long Do Lemon Law Cases Take If You Own a Used Car?

Fortunately, the California lemon law covers used cars as well as new and leased cars. But you must have one of three warranties to qualify. These include:

  • Transferred new car factory warranty
  • Certified Pre-Owned (CPO) warranty
  • Lemon Law Buyback warranty

How Do I Start the Lemon Law Process?

If you purchased or leased a defective vehicle that is still under the original manufacturer’s warranty, you may be entitled to have your vehicle repurchased or replaced under California’s lemon law. But, in order to assert your rights effectively, you need to have a clear understanding of the steps involved in filing a lemon law claim. Our California lemon lawyer identifies the following steps:

  • Take Your Vehicle to the Dealership for Repair.
  • Get Your Dealership Repair Orders.
  • Give the Dealership a “Reasonable Number of Attempts” to Resolve the Issue.
  • Gather All Relevant Documentation.
  • Your Car Warranty Does Not Need to be Current.
  • Hire an Experienced Lemon Lawyer.
  • A Demand Letter is Not Required.
  • Negotiate a Settlement or Litigate.
  • Arbitration is Not Required.
  • Proceed to Trial.

In conclusion, Lemon Law helps consumers file claims for damages as a result of defective products. The Lemon Law was created to allow consumers with defective products to direct their frustrations to one place. By allowing consumers to re-route their frustrations to the manufacturer or distributor, this act relieves the consumers of the stress of dealing with multiple companies. The Lemon Law also strengthens the consumer’s bargaining position, because manufacturers and distributors know that consumers will still buy their products despite defects. If the consumer can prove that the defect substantially affects the vehicle’s performance, it’s considered a lemon.
