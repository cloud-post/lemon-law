How California's Used Car Lemon Law Protects Buyers:



The main question is whether the lemon law applies to used cars in California. If it does, the answer depends on the characteristics specified by California.

California law covers a buyer who has purchased a car with warranty featuring a substantial defect or one that cannot be made free from defects following a certain number of repairs.

You bought the car from a dealer or retailer, not a private citizen; furthermore, the car must possess any remaining manufacturer's warranty to qualify under California law because the diagnosis could be considered a lemon.

California laws concerning used car lemon laws mandate that the manufacturer or dealer must repurchase or repair the damaged car if it cannot be repaired or there is an unresolved flaw.

Maximizing Warranties on Used Vehicles in CA:

California's used car prior sale ability laws apply to used cars with warranty coverage. The vehicle may be traded second-hand however still under the maker's original warranty. Furthermore, the dealer could have provided a dealer warranty.

According to the California Lemon Law, you can buy a used car that was covered under the manufacturer's warranty or that was given a dealer warranty, but you have to be mindful of your personal automobile purchase.

Implied Car Warranties:

Unless the dealer sold you a car as-is, your car is covered by an implied warranty of merchantability, regardless of whether it has an unsigned warranty.

The implied warranty of merchantability is an unwritten guarantee from the manufacturer that the vehicle will fulfill its function. If there has been a significant flaw in your used vehicle, and you think it existed until you purchased it, the seller might have acted in violation of the warranty.

Do a quick check to learn about whether your vehicle is a lemon:

Deciding whether a used car is a good chance depends on whether your car displays one or more of these qualities: 

  • After a number of attempts at repair, the problem remains.
  • Your vehicle consistently fails in quality and performance.
  • The car has a serious problem in its safety.
  • The damaged (used) vehicle visited a repair shop for a long time (more than 30 days).

If you suspect that your vehicle is a lemon, you need to allow a certain amount of time for the manufacturer or dealer to repair the vehicle. Your car is likely a lemon vehicle if it's been in the shop for 30 days or more or has not been repaired despite multiple repair attempts.
