Rights Under Lemon Law:



Lemon law stipulates that a manufacturer defects or has to fix a replacement vehicle if the owner finishes the warranty period of a lemon vehicle. Lemon law refers to the Lemon Law of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act of 1975, which declares protections for individuals if they hold a lemon vehicle.

If an automobile has critical repairs or requires a succession of maintenance and if the car's warranty period has ended, it's known as a lemon. Lemon Law is a reference to the law in the U.S. that seeks to ensure purchasers' security from automobile defects.

Lemon law can be applied to vehicles of any make like a car, truck, van, SUV, motorcycle, boat, and so on. If any of these vehicles are found to be defective, then the consumer can be awarded either a refund, a replacement, or a cash settlement. To qualify under state "lemon laws," the defect must be substantial and must have occurred within a certain time period or mileage limit, usually 12,000 miles.

Motor vehicle dealers are by law obligated to inform you of your rights under the Lemon Law in your location. They also need to notify you whether the automobile you wish to purchase has ever been turned around under the Lemon Law (Lemon Buyback). A vehicle that has been returned by the dealer to the manufacturer is a Lemon Buyback. Such lemon purchase backs are often offered in auction.
