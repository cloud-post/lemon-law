Know Your Rights When It Comes To Lemon Laws For Used Cars


Whenever you buy a car in California, you automatically receive certain protections under the law. These protections are there to help you in case you end up with a lemon. But what happens when you buy a used car? Are you still protected?

The answer is yes! California's lemon law applies to used cars as well as new cars. So if you buy a used car and it turns out to be a lemon, you can still get a refund or replacement.

Qualifying under the Lemon Law

California law was updated in 2013 to require that California buy here, pay here used car dealers must give at least 30 days or 1,000 miles of basic repairs for any vehicle sold or purchased. However, buy here, pay here dealers will provide a better warranty, especially if the automobile has been certified pre-owned. In either case, the Lemon Law affords you compensation if you purchased a used car and the following criteria are satisfied:

  • You purchased the car from a customer rather than a private individual. Private car sales are not covered by public laws.
  • On the car, there's an active manufacturer's warranty period or an extended warranty offered by a retailer.
  • The used car has a lot of issues.
  • The pre-owned vehicle has spent too much time at the repair shop, being repaired.
  • Despite a reasonable number of repair attempts, the car is still not fixed and the problem still persists.

Types of Used Vehicles Covered

A used vehicle must have an active warranty to qualify for legal protection under the law. Even if the warranty isn't the official manufacturer's warranty, a used car dealer's express warranty will suffice. Lemon vehicles, which the manufacturer repurchased as buybacks and resold with new express written warranties covering the defect, may also be covered under the provisions of the CA Lemon Laws.

The Law’s Used Car Guidelines are Less Specific

Some limitations on lemon law, which generally involve new vehicle purchases, are not specifically spelled out when it comes to used car purchases. As such, the lemon law case for used car purchases can be more complicated and difficult to resolve than it would be for brand-new vehicle purchases. To give an example, in the event of a manufacturer being unable to fix a sold vehicle problem within a reasonable number of repair attempts, the manufacturer must provide the buyer with a replacement vehicle or a full refund of the purchase, minus an amount that can be attributed to the consumer's use of the vehicle before the defect was discovered. In the case of buying a new automobile, the authorities provide a method for calculating this fee. With used auto purchases, however, such a formula is not offered, and so this method is left to the contending parties to determine an agreeable cost.

How Many Repair Attempts Must Be Made?

It is actually contingent on the type of defect the vehicle has. For a critical issue that endangers the safety of the vehicle and the safety of its passengers, the manufacturer has to make sure that the car is only deemed defective once or twice before it is decided to meet the minimum safety standard. This would take place since the problem would be in the car's brake system, steering system, transmission, engine, or any other essential part of a car. If a mechanic is not able to resolve the vehicle's issues after several attempts, Lemon Law requires them to reimburse the owner.

If a car has an impending dangerous defect that can cause death or serious bodily harm, one or 2 repair attempts are considered reasonable before compensation is required.

If four repair attempts have been made and the problem persists and has not been dealt with (even when it's not life-threatening), then the standard has been met and compensation is required.

If the car has been in the shop for repairs for 30 days or more, and there is one defect or multiple defects still not able to be repaired, then the threshold value has been reached.

In conclusion, the CA lemon law has specific and documented rules that protect consumers. The law requires car makers to fix a car if it breaks down due to manufacturing defects or safety defects, or if a car has a defect that significantly impairs its ability to meet safety or performance standards.
