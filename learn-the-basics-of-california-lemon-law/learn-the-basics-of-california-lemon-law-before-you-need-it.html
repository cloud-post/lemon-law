Learn The Basics Of California Lemon Law Before You Need It


The lemon law is a state law that gives consumers the right to receive a refund or replacement product from a business if the product does not meet their expectations. The law covers a wide range of products, including cars, appliances, and furniture. This article provides an overview of the lemon law in California, including what types of products are covered and how to file a complaint.

What Counts as a “Lemon” Under the Lemon Car Law in California?

How a settlement is judged under the lemon law varies on the situation in California.

The vehicle must be inspected according to several requirements to qualify as a lemon, including that.

  • The warranty must have been purchased with a "express and active" policy.
  • Cars purchased from private parties are not covered by the manufacturer lemon law.
  • Reasonable number of remedied repairs. The manufacturer or dealer needed to have been allowed to make multiple proposed remedies.
  • The issue with the vehicle should be considered a material failure, meaning that it substantially diminishes the vehicle's ability to function.

It is not unusual for a manufacturer to declare that car defects, which arise from the owner not taking care of the vehicle appropriately, cannot be classified as a lemon. Call an attorney if you can conclude that your vehicle's defect does not fall under the California lemon law.

Our Rights as Consumers

When customers face problems when buying defective goods, they're often confused about their rights.

In a couple of instances, we just keep the product and buy a new one at our own cost and blame ourselves for purchasing a defective one. The California Lemon Laws are designed to protect us against faulty products and we are ready to receive monetary reimbursement for our losses.

The California lemon laws allow a variety of solutions to fix lemon vehicle owners.

  • Repurchase of the defective car 
  • Replacement of the defective car; or
  • Cash settlement; and
  • Payment of the consumer’s reasonable attorneys fees and costs

In addition, if a manufacturer willfully violated California's lemon law, an owner of that particular vehicle may be able to sue the manufacturer for damages.
